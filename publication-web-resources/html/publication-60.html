Martin Heidegger’s concept of “being-in-the-world” (In-der-Welt-sein) is a central idea in his existential philosophy, particularly in his seminal work Being and Time. This concept captures the fundamental way in which human beings exist. Heidegger challenges the traditional Cartesian view that separates the subject (the thinking self) from the object (the external world). Instead, he argues that humans are not isolated entities detached from the world but are always already immersed in it. This immersion is what he describes as “being-in-the-world.”

Heidegger asserts that our existence is inherently relational. We are always situated in a context, engaging with the world around us through our activities, interactions, and perceptions. This interconnectedness means

that our identity and existence cannot be fully understood in isolation but are shaped by our relationships with the world and others within it. “Being-in-the-world” involves care, which is not merely a feeling but a fundamental structure of existence. Care denotes the way in which we are concerned with our own being and the being of others. Additionally, “being-with” (Mitsein) highlights that we are always in the company of others, even if not physically present, as the world we navigate is a shared one.

Heidegger differentiates between authentic and inauthentic modes of being. Authenticity involves embracing one’s own being-in-the-world and making choices that reflect one’s true self, rather than simply conforming to societal norms or expectations. In contrast, inauthenticity arises when individuals lose themselves in the “they” (das Man), a term Heidegger uses to describe the impersonal collective norms that can dictate our behavior.

The interconnectedness highlighted in “being-in-the-world” suggests that benevolence, or concern for the well-being of others, is not merely a moral option but a structural necessity for authentic existence. Since our being is always already connected with others, the well-being of others and the environment is inherently linked to our own. Thus, the ethical commitment to benevolence becomes a natural extension of recognizing our being-in-the-world.

Cultivating Benevolence Recognizing the importance of Benevolence is just the first step. Cultivating it requires conscious effort and practice. We’ll discuss such techniques in depth in Book 4. In the meantime, here are some strategies for developing Benevolence:

Expand Circles of Empathy: Regularly challenge yourself to consider the perspectives and needs of those outside your immediate circle. This practice helps broaden our understanding of diverse experiences and encourages a more inclusive approach to life. Start by imagining the daily lives of people in different circumstances—a struggling single parent, a refugee, or an elderly person living alone. Gradually extend this empathy to other species and ecosystems, recognizing their intrinsic value and right to flourish.
