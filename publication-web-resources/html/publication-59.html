Deontological Ethics emphasizes the importance of duty and adherence to rules. In this framework, the morality of an action is determined by whether it aligns with a set of principles or rules, regardless of the consequences. Actions are considered morally right if they follow these duties or rules, such as acting in a way that could be universally applied.

Utilitarianism is a consequentialist framework that evaluates the morality of actions based on their outcomes. The central idea is that an action is morally right if it maximizes overall happiness or pleasure and minimizes pain or suffering. This approach focuses on achieving “the greatest good for the greatest number,” making it highly outcome-oriented.

Virtue Ethics focuses on the character and virtues of the moral agent rather than on specific actions or their consequences. According to this framework, moral behavior arises from the development of good character traits, such as courage, temperance, and wisdom. The emphasis is on living a life of moral excellence by cultivating virtues that lead to human flourishing.

Care Ethics highlights the importance of relationships, care, and empathy in ethical decision-making. It challenges the impartiality and abstraction found in other ethical frameworks, arguing that moral decisions should be based on the context of relationships and the needs of others, especially those who are vulnerable. This approach prioritizes nurturing and maintaining connections with others and responding to the specific circumstances of individuals.
