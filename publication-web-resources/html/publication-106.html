George’s Ideas In the Modern Frontiers of Land and Capital The dawn of AGI necessitates a radical reimagining of George’s principles, expanding our conception of “land” to encompass the digital realms that increasingly define our economic landscape. In this evolved paradigm, land transcends physical boundaries, incorporating:

Traditional real estate and natural resources

Digital platforms and virtual spaces of economic activity

Data reservoirs that fuel AI systems and drive value creation

AI and robotics infrastructure, including computational power and algorithmic frameworks

As AGI promises to revolutionize production across sectors, potentially supplanting human labor on an unprecedented scale, we find ourselves gravitating towards an economy primarily composed of this expanded notion of land and capital. This shift amplifies the prescience of George’s warnings about the perils of concentrated ownership.

In the age of AGI, we might ask: what would a “Monopoly” game look like? Such a game could serve as powerful education-al tool, helping people understand the economic dynamics of the modern world. Instead of properties like Boardwalk or Park Place, players might compete for control of data centers, AI algo-rithms, or global attention spans. Chance cards could involve vi-ral social media events or breakthrough AI innovations, while Community Chest might represent open-source projects.

Conversely, what would a game illustrating the Alignment Economy look like?

THE ALIGNMENT ECONOMY

213

The monopolization of digital “land” by tech behemoths echoes the land baronies of the 19th century, but with far-reaching implications that George could scarcely have imagined. An entity controlling an AGI system, our collective attention and data, wields influence that dwarfs that of historical land magnates, threatening to reshape the very fabric of our economic and social structures.

The impending transformation of the labor market by AGI compels us to grapple with fundamental questions about value, ownership, and distribution in this new era. We must contemplate novel economic models that recognize the intrinsic worth of human existence beyond traditional notions of productivity. In a world where algorithms and robots may perform much of what we now consider “work,” how do we ensure human flourishing? How do we distribute the unprecedented wealth that AGI-driven productivity gains promise?

The answers to these questions may lie in reimagining our economic foundations, much as George did in his time. By expanding our understanding of value to encompass the uniquely human capacities for creativity, empathy, and consciousness, we open the door to economic systems that honor the inherent dignity of every individual. In doing so, we may find that the most valuable human contribution in the age of AGI is simply being human.

The Alignment Economy is an attempt at such reimagining.
