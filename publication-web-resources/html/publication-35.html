AGI radically upends the employment and labor markets as it subsumes jobs and tasks across virtually all industries and sectors. As AGI systems become more capable, an increasingly artificial workforce may render many forms of human labor obsolete.

If left unchecked, it would likely further exacerbate economic inequality as immense wealth accrues to those who own the AGI systems, capital, and land. Furthermore, in the absence of ethical guidelines and regulations, individuals and corporations may prioritize profit over human well-being and environmental sustainability.

The CEO of Your Own Life: Imagine a world where every individual becomes the CEO of their own life, equipped with an infinitely capable AI workforce. This utopian vision suggests an economy of phenomenal productivity and abundance, with the potential to lift humanity out of scarcity. However, in such a landscape, the distribution of success is likely to follow a power-law, with wealth and resources increasingly concentrated in the hands of a few. Universal basic income (UBI) has been proposed as a potential solution to address the challenges of job displacement and income inequality. UBI would provide every citizen with a guaranteed income, regardless of their employment status. This would provide a safety net for those displaced by automation, while also empowering individuals to pursue their passions, start their own businesses, or engage in further education and training.

Exploring the Pros and Cons of UBI

Pros: Provides a safety net for those displaced by automation. Empowers individuals to pursue their passions and interests. Reduces poverty and inequality. Stimulates the economy by providing individuals with more disposable income.

Cons: High cost of implementation (although this will not be a problem in a much more productive society). Potential disincentive to work (again, this will no longer be an issue). Potential for inflation (this can be managed by policies like restricting borrowing against future income). Ethical concerns about creating a culture of dependency.

This prompts a broader perspective on UBI—not as a mere handout, but as a venture investment from society into individual potential. Alternatively, venture capital could evolve to embrace greater experimentation, casting wider nets and taking on the risks of nurturing untapped human talent. Culturally, we may come to view basic income and venture capital as asymptotically convergent—both serving as mechanisms for providing financial support to unlock latent potentials that might otherwise go unre-alized, thereby opening new avenues for growth and creativity.
