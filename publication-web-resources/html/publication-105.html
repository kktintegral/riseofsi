These insights compel us to reimagine George’s principles for the digital age, radically expanding our concept of “land” to encompass the intangible yet invaluable resources that increasingly drive our economy. In this new paradigm, we must grapple with how to equitably distribute the immense value generated by our collective digital footprint and cognitive bandwidth. The modern equivalent of “land” now includes:

Physical Assets: Traditional real estate, natural resources, and energy infrastructure.

Cognitive Capital: Human attention, creativity, and well-being—increasingly commodified by digital platforms.

Data Ecosystems: Vast reservoirs of information that fuel AI systems and drive innovation.

Digital Infrastructure: The underlying technologies and networks that enable our interconnected world.

Algorithmic Capabilities: The sophisticated AI and machine learning models that power automated decision-making and value creation.

This expanded definition challenges us to develop new economic models that recognize and fairly allocate the value derived from these diverse forms of “land.” Just as George sought to address the inequities of land ownership in his time, we must now confront the concentration of power and wealth in the hands of those who control these modern resources.

As AGI promises to revolutionize production across sectors, potentially supplanting human labor on an unprecedented scale, we face an urgent need to reconceptualize our economic framework. An entity controlling AGI systems, our collective attention, and data wields influence that eclipses that of historical land magnates, poised to reshape the very fabric of our economic and social structures.

The answers may lie in boldly reimagining our economic foundations, much as George did in his time. By expanding our understanding of value to fully encompass human qualities— such as creativity, empathy, and consciousness—we can craft economic systems that honor the inherent dignity of every individual. In this emerging paradigm, we may discover that the most valuable human contribution is simply being human.

The Alignment Economy represents an attempt at such reimagining.

Monopoly: From Critique to Cultural Phenomenon

The enduring relevance of George’s ideas is evident in the amus-ing history of the board game Monopoly. Originally conceived as “The Landlord’s Game” by Elizabeth Magie, its purpose was to illustrate the negative social consequences of land monopolization—a core tenet of George’s philosophy. While the game evolved into a popular pastime, its origins highlight the persistent concern surrounding ownership concentration and its impact on economic equity.
