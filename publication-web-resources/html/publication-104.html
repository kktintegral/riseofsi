The Paradox of Progress and Poverty

George’s seminal 1879 treatise, Progress and Poverty, addressed a paradox that persists today: why does poverty endure alongside economic progress? He argued that while technological ad-vancements and increased productivity generate wealth, they often intensify inequality. The bene-fits, George observed, accrue disproportionately to landowners, leaving the masses facing poverty and dwindling opportunities. This disparity stems from the concentration of land ownership and its associated economic rent in the hands of a few.

In the digital age, we witness a striking parallel to George’s observations. As tech giants amass ex-traordinary wealth and power, many workers grapple with economic insecurity and diminishing prospects. The modern equivalent of “land” now extends beyond physical property to include in-tangible assets like human attention and data—re-sources increasingly captured and monetized by digital platforms and AI systems. This represents the new frontier of economic value capture.

The Land Value Tax: An Economic Reformation George’s proposed solution, a tax focused on land value, was a radical departure from conventional economic thought. This tax, he argued, would capture the unearned economic rent derived from land ownership, effectively socializing land’s value while leaving wages and capital untouched. It would generate revenue for public goods and services, incentivize productive land use, curb speculation, and ultimately alleviate poverty by ensuring a fairer distribution of progress’s benefits. However, this proposal is not without its challenges.
