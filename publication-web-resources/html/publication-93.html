The process of price determination in the Alignment Economy is not a black box, but an interactive dialogue between human stakeholders and Superintelligent systems. This approach combines machine learning methods, such as value function estimation, with component aggregation techniques to arrive at comprehensive pricing models.

Crucially, Superintelligence doesn’t simply dictate prices. Instead, it presents its calculations transparently, explaining the reasoning behind each factor considered. Human participants can then scrutinize these explanations, offer feedback, and suggest adjustments based on additional qualitative and quantitative factors.

This iterative process ensures that pricing remains aligned with human values and intuitions while benefiting from the vast analytical capabilities of Superintelligence. It creates a system of continuous learning and refinement, where both human and artificial intelligences contribute to increasingly expressive economic models.

To better understand how the calculation can be done, we can draw a parallel with the value functions in machine learning. Value functions help us figure out how good it is to take a particular action in an environment, considering future consequences.

Consider the concept of Q-learning, a reinforcement learning technique where an agent learns to associate actions with their long-term consequences. Imagine observing an agent navigating its environment, taking actions, and experiencing the resulting rewards or penalties. As we watch, we see the agent gradually refining its understanding of the world, building an internal model—a “Q-table”—that assigns a value to each action in each possible state. Over time, the agent learns to make choices that maximize its long-term rewards, navigating its environment with increasing skill. In practice this Q-function is a learned model, rather than a simple scoreboard, that can generalize to unseen states of the world and actions.

In the Alignment Economy, we can think of prices as analogous to this Q-function. The price of an action in a given context represents how well it will condition the world towards Freedom, taking into account all stakeholders’ preferences and expected potential future implications. It seeks to understand how actions impact our collective—not by following pre-programmed ethical rules, but by analyzing the vast network of human behavior, preferences, societal, and real-world consequences. Just as in reinforcement learning, where the challenge lies in approximating the true value function, our task in the Alignment Economy is to approximate these true alignment prices. It’s a form of “Q-learning for ethics,” where the “rewards” are advancements in our collective well-being. The algorithm for doing this will be developed further in Book 2.

A key feature of the Alignment Economy is the rejection of universal fungibility in pricing. Prices are a function of total action and the full context of what’s known about the world, making them inherently context-dependent. This non-fungibility means that identical products or services may be priced differently based on the current state of the world and the broader implications of the transaction. Furthermore, since actions modify the world state, they are non-commutative and their effects cannot be naively summed.

The same product or service might be priced differently based on factors such as the buyer’s intent, the potential impact of the transaction, or the broader societal context at the time. For exam-ple, a tool bought for a community project might be priced lower than the same tool purchased for personal use. Or a luxury item might be priced higher during times of resource scarcity to discourage frivolous consumption.

Crucially, this AGI doesn’t stop at calculations. It translates its analysis into explanations that resonate with human understand-ing. It assigns a price that reflects the multifaceted value of an action, considering both immediate impacts and long-term consequences for our collective freedoms.
