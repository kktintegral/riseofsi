Freedom as the Ideal Agent: we can view Freedom as the state of an ideal agent with maximum agency—one that is most effective across all relevant timescales and contexts.

Evolution and Freedom While traditional evolutionary theory focuses on the survival of genes, the Free Energy Principle suggests a broader view. Genes cannot be separated from their context; they are better viewed as “context editors” that influence the broader system in which they exist.

Evolution favors systems that can better predict and adapt to their environment—in other words, systems with greater freedom.

Knowledge: By continuously updating internal models, systems increase their knowledge and understanding of the world, paralleling our pillar of Knowledge in Freedom.

Power: The ability to accurately predict and influence one’s environment enhances a system’s capacity to act effectively, resonating with our notion of Power.

Vitality: Successful minimization of free energy allows systems to maintain their integrity and thrive, aligning with our concept of Vitality.

Benevolence: While not explicitly part of Friston’s original formulation, Benevolence emerges naturally when an agent identifies with the broader context in which it exists. This alignment occurs when systems must consider their wider environment to effectively minimize free energy over multiple time scales.
