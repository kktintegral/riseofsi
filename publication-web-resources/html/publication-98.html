Cooperative Convergence Through Shared Goals

The idea that an ecosystem of Super-intelligent agents would naturally converge in their decision-making processes finds strong support in Aumann’s agreement theorem, a fundamental result in game theory with profound implications for our model.

Aumann’s agreement theorem, formulat-ed by mathematician Robert Aumann in 1976, states that two rational agents with common priors cannot “agree to disagree.” More specifically, if two rational agents have the same prior beliefs and their posterior beliefs are common knowledge, then these posterior beliefs must be identical. This holds true even if the agents have different information.

In the context of our ecosystem of Super-intelligent agents, Aumann’s theorem sug-gests that:

Inevitable Consensus: Given their shared goal of maximizing Freedom and their rational nature, our Super-intelligent agents would inevitably reach a consensus on matters crucial to the Alignment Economy, such as pricing and resource allocation.

Efficient Information Exchange: The theorem implies that disagreements between agents would prompt further information exchange. This process would continue until all relevant information is shared and a consensus is reached.

Common Knowledge as a Catalyst: As the agents make their beliefs common knowledge (which they are incentivized to do to maximize Freedom), this very act drives them towards agreement.

Overcoming Individual Limitations: Even if individual agents have limited or differing information, the process of reaching agreement allows the collective to transcend individual limitations, approaching omniscience as a group.

The implications of Aumann’s theorem for our Alignment Economy are profound:

Rapid Convergence: Super-intelligent agents, with their vast computational power and ability to exchange information rapidly, would likely reach agreement far more quickly than human actors.

Dynamic Equilibrium: As new information becomes available, the agents would swiftly update their beliefs and reach new consensuses, creating a dynamic equilibrium that rapidly adapts to changing circumstances.

Trustworthiness of Collective Decisions: The theorem provides a theoretical basis for trusting the collective decisions of the ecosystem. If rational, Freedom-maximizing agents agree on a course of action, we can have high confidence in its alignment with our shared goals.

Resilience Against Manipulation: It becomes exceedingly difficult for any single agent to manipulate the system, as disagreements would trigger information exchange that exposes any attempts at deception.
