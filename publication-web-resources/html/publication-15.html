Collaborative robots, or “co-bots,” can safely work along-side humans, opening doors for applications in diverse fields like manufacturing, logistics, and even agriculture. Robots are no longer confined to factories, venturing into areas like health-care, with surgical robots assist-ing in delicate procedures.

The evolution of humanoid robots has seen several key milestones:

WABOT-1, introduced at Waseda University in Tokyo in 1973, marked the inception of humanoid robotics over 50 years ago.

ASIMO by Honda, launched in the early 2000s, represented another significant milestone with its advanced mobility, recognition, and interaction capabilities.

The concept of humanoid automata possesses a rich and ancient history, with roots extending across diverse cultures. Earliest references emerge from 4th century BCE Greek mythology and philosophical treatises originating in China. Later, tangible manifestations of these ideas appeared through the ingenuity of inventors across the Middle East, Italy, Japan, and France.

Now Humanoids are moving so fast with many developments at companies including Tesla, 1X, Boston Dynamics, etc as well as several Chinese companies.

Latest advances in foundation models including language models (LLMs) and vision models (including video understanding and generation) are poised to change robotics.

Earlier work by various researchers and companies, showcase teleoperation or basic imitation learning in robots. My company, for example, has built the world’s first commercial imitation learning system. Having explored these initial methods and understood their limits, we’ve cemented our confidence that the key is to develop and scale foundation World Models (WMs). These models bridge generative AI with the physical realm. WMs will enable general-purpose capabilities in the real world.

Currently the US lags behind in robotics. This is primarily due to the history of US offshoring manufacturing to countries like China and Mexico. Japan produces about half of the world’s industrial robots, so I moved to Tokyo and built the company from the ground up in two bases, San Francisco and Tokyo, bridging the two premier hubs in AI and robotics. In Europe, Munich is a great place, but so is the entire corridor from Switzerland (Zurich), to Sweden (Gothenburg), and notably Denmark (Odense).

While humanoid robots may capture our imagination with their anthropomorphic design, the real transformative potential lies in robots that can design and construct other robots. Rather than a world populated with one class of robots, in the future robots will make robots through a combination of additive and subtractive manufacturing, enabling a cambrian explosion of robot embodiments (including a wide variety of humanoid and non-humanoid types). The technology and infrastructure necessary to enable robots to make robots is being developed and it represents a key step towards mass customization, diversity, efficiency and scalability.
