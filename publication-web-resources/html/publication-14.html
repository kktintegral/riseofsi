Humankind’s fascination with mechanical servants and artificial beings stretches back centuries. Across cultures, myths and legends described automatons capable of independent movement and action.

In ancient Greece, there were tales of Hephaestus, the god of fire and metalworking, crafting wondrous devices.

China’s rich history boasts inven-tions like the “指南车” (zhǐ nán chē), a south-pointing chariot no matter how the chariot turns, hinting at early forays into auto-mated machinery. Yan Shi in the 3rd century BCE are said to have created lifelike automatons.

The quintessential Renaissance polymath, Leonardo da Vinci, left behind detailed sketches for mechanical creations, including an armored knight capable of movement.

Jacques de Vaucanson from France constructed elaborate automatons like a writing machine and a remarkably lifelike duck.

Advancements in microprocessors and computing power in the latter half of the 20th century paved the way for more sophisticated robots. Research labs across the globe witnessed the creation of mobile robots capable of navigating their environments.

The mid-20th century marked a pivotal shift with the inven-tion of the first programmable industrial robot. Developed by George Devol and Joseph Engelberger in the 1950s, the Unimate robotic arm revo-lutionized manufacturing by automating repetitive and potentially dangerous tasks.

Examples include the Stanford Cart, an early remote-controlled rover with a camera, and Shakey the Robot, a mobile robot able to reason about its actions. While these early iterations were not yet widespread, they were critical steps in the evolution of mobile robotics.
