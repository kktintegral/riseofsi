Realism

The Pursuit of Power and Survival

In the study of international relations, two dominant theories have shaped our understanding of how nations interact on the global stage: Realism and Liberalism. These theories have been championed by influential political scientists, who have provided significant insights into the motivations and behaviors of states.

Realism is rooted in a pessimistic view of human nature and the anarchic structure of the international system. Realists argue that the international arena is inherently com-petitive and conflict-prone, where the primary goal of each nation-state is survival. This is driven by the belief that in a world where no central authority exists to enforce rules or protect states, the security dilemma reigns supreme—each state must rely on its own capabilities to ensure its survival.

One prominent proponent of Realism is John Mearsheimer, a political scientist and professor at the University of Chicago. Mearsheimer’s work is primarily associated with the theory of offensive realism, which posits that great powers are inherently driven to seek dom-inance over their rivals to achieve security. In his seminal work, “The Tragedy of Great Power Politics,” Mearsheimer argues that the international system compels states to pursue power aggressively, as the accumulation of power is the best way to ensure their survival in an anarchic world. According to Mearsheimer, conflict and competi-tion are inevitable as states strive to achieve security and prevent others from gaining power at their expense.

Liberalism

The Promise of Cooperation and Progress

In contrast, Liberalism offers a more optimistic view of international relations. It posits that, despite the anarchy of the international system, cooperation between states is not only possible but beneficial. Liberalism is grounded in the belief that human nature is not entirely self-serving or power-hungry; rather, individuals and, by extension, states can work together for mutual benefit.

One of the leading voices in Liberal thought is Francis Fukuyama, a political scientist and economist who is best known for his book “The End of History and the Last Man.” Fukuyama argues that the spread of liberal democracy and free-market capitalism represents the endpoint of humanity’s ideological evolution. He believes that liberal democracy is the most stable and just form of government, one that facilitates international cooperation and promotes peace. Fukuyama’s liberalism is centered on the idea that democratic institutions, economic interdependence, and the rule of law can create a more peaceful and prosperous global order. Unlike Realism, which often views conflict as inevitable, Fukuyama’s Liberalism holds that the spread of democracy and human rights can mitigate the inherent risks of international anarchy, leading to a more stable and cooperative international system.
