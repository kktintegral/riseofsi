Asimov’s Three Laws: A Founda-tion for Robot Ethics In 1942, science fiction author Isaac Asimov introduced the Three Laws of Robotics in his short story “Runaround.” These laws have since become a cornerstone of robot ethics discussions:

A robot may not injure a human being or, through inaction, allow a human being to come to harm.

A robot must obey the orders given it by human beings except where such orders would conflict with the First Law.

A robot must protect its own exis-tence as long as such protection does not conflict with the First or Second Laws.

However, these laws have faced criticism for their limitations. They assume a clear distinction between humans and robots, don’t account for complex moral dilemmas, and could potential-ly lead to unintended consequences. For instance, a strict inter-pretation of the First Law could paralyze a robot in a world full of potential human harm. Moreover, these laws don’t address the ethical challenges posed by more advanced AI systems, particu-larly of Superintelligence.

Given these limitations, it’s clear that we need a more nuanced and comprehensive framework to guide the development of advanced AI systems, particularly as we approach the era of Superintelligence.
