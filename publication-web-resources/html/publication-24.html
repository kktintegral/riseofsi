A crucial point: the extent of neutrality of a media platform’s curation activity is directly tied to the level of control it grants its users. The act of curating feeds from an almost infinite pool of content is, in essence, a form of creation. This could place liability on the platform, especially when users are passive and the platform has incentive to maximize consumption. Conversely, when users actively search for specific content, the liability shifts towards the content creators and, potentially, the users themselves. There’s a broad spectrum between passive consumption and active selection. By empowering users to customize their feeds based on their intentions, recommender systems can minimize platform liability.

Focusing on user empowerment, rather than solely maximizing short-term engagement, is aligned with social well-being and is ultimately the long-term winning strategy for social media platform companies. Platforms that prioritize user autonomy while balancing societal concerns are likely to build stronger, more sustainable user bases and better navigate legal challenges in the long run.

As we move forward, we have an opportunity to harness the power of AI to create social media ecosystems that enrich our lives and strengthen our communities, rather than divide and exploit them.
