Advances in computing power, coupled with the increasing availability of vast data-sets, marked a turning point for AI. The focus shifted to machine learning techniques that enabled systems to learn patterns from data rather than rely solely on hand-coded rules. Breakthroughs in computer vision, like the de-velopment of convolutional neural networks, led to improved image recognition capabili-ties. Iconic moments like IBM’s Deep Blue de-feating chess grandmaster Garry Kasparov in 1997 highlighted the progress made in AI’s ability to tackle complex strategic problems.

The Birth of a Field (1950s - 1960s): The Dartmouth Summer Re-search Project on Artificial Intelligence, held in 1956, is often consid-ered the founding event of AI as a formal field of study. This workshop brought together pioneering researchers like John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, and Herbert Simon, who coined the term “Artificial Intelli-gence.” This period saw the development of early AI programs capa-ble of playing checkers, proving mathematical theorems, and under-standing natural language to a limited degree. The initial enthusiasm faced setbacks due to limitations in computing power and the inabil-ity of early AI systems to generalize knowledge. Funding for AI re-search dwindled, leading to what’s known as the “AI Winter.” Howev-er, behind the scenes, the 1980s saw the rise of expert systems, rule-based AI programs capable of replicating the decision-making abilities of humans in specialized domains like medical diagnosis.

Deep Learning and the Narrow AI Boom (2010s): The early 2010s witnessed a revolution in AI with the advent and refinement of deep learning, a type of machine learning leveraging multi-layered artificial neural networks. Combined with massive datasets and powerful GPUs (Graphics Processing Units), deep learning enabled significant leaps in speech recognition, computer vision, and natural language processing. Achievements like Google’s AlphaGo defeat-ing world champion Go player Lee Sedol in 2016 showcased AI sur-passing human abilities in strategic games once thought impossible.

THE IMPERATIVE OF A SHARED VISION

21

Artificial intelligence (AI) has come a long way since its early days. AI systems today drive applications like self-driving cars, facial recog-nition systems, language translation tools, and much more. We have routine deployments of sophisticated systems that excel at specific tasks, from recognizing faces to playing com-plex games like chess and Go. However, these systems, known as narrow AI, lack the general intelligence that humans possess.

Large language multimodal foundation models (LLMs)

The Scaling of Foundation Models Era (2020-present): advancements in foundation models, such as large language models (LLMs) and multimodal foundation models, are seen by some as potential stepping stones towards AGI. These models are trained on massive datasets and demonstrate remarkable abilities in language understanding, generation, and knowledge retrieval. Their ability to translate, write different kinds of creative text, and answer your questions in an informative way (even if they hallucinate sometimes) hints at a more generalized intelligence than previous narrow AI systems. While foundation models alone do not equate to AGI, their ability to adapt and learn across different tasks suggests progress toward that goal.
