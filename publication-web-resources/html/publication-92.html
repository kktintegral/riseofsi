The Alignment Economy faces a central challenge: quantifying how specific actions align with our collective pursuit of Freedom. This task demands a system capable of bridging the gap between the abstract ideal of Freedom and the concrete realities of human choices and their consequences. In this context, AGI functions not as a simple calculator, but as a sophisticated interpreter, weaving together data-driven insights with ethical reasoning to arrive at prices that reflect both human values and the long-term implications of our actions.

The Complexity of Ethical Decision-Making Contempo-rary moral philosophy and decision theory aim to provide a framework for ethical decision-making centered on value maximi-zation. This involves evaluating the consequences of all possible outcomes and weighting them by their likelihoods. The idea is to pursue various paths proportionally based on their potential value, with effort scaled by expected impact and adjusted for uncertain-ty. However, this comprehensive approach is often uncomputable in practice, making it difficult to operationalize.

The Alignment Economy recognizes that human values cannot be simply summed or averaged. Instead, it proposes that these diverse and often conflicting values should be arbitrated through the lens of Freedom.

The AGI achieves this by fusing distinct threads: the high-level abstraction of Freedom, representing our shared aspirations for a flourishing future, and the intricate web of human preferences, reflecting the nuanced values and desires of individuals and communities. However, it’s important to realize that these “prices” in the Alignment Economy are not directly computable in any straightforward manner. This concept bears some resemblance to Kolmogorov complexity in computer science and information theory.

Kolmogorov complexity, named after So-viet mathematician Andrey Kolmogorov, refers to the length of the shortest computer program that can produce a given string as output. In essence, it measures the “complex-ity” of a string by the size of the most concise description of that string. For instance, a string of all zeros would have a low Kolmogorov complexity, as it can be described simply as “print 0 n times,” where n is the length of the string. In contrast, a completely random string would have a high Kolmogorov complexity, as there’s no shorter way to describe it than to list out all its characters.

An important aspect of Kolmogorov complexity is that while we can conceptually agree that there exists a minimum descrip-tion length for any given string, finding that exact length is provably impossible for arbi-trary strings. This is due to the halting prob-lem in computer science, which demon-strates that no algorithm can determine whether any given program will eventually halt or run forever.

Similarly, in the Alignment Economy, we posit that there exists a “true price” that perfectly quantifies the alignment of any action with our pursuit of Freedom. However, directly computing this price is as elusive as calculating the exact Kolmogorov complexity of an arbitrary string.

Yet, this apparent limitation doesn’t render the concept useless. Just as we can develop algorithms that bound and approximate Kolmogorov complexity and provide useful insights about the complexity of strings, we can create mechanisms in the Alignment Economy that serve as proxy objectives for discovering these alignment prices.
