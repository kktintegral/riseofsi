FREEDOM AS A UNIFYING IDEAL Both Realism and Liberalism have limitations in explaining international relations. Realism, with its focus on power and survival, can be overly deterministic, assuming that conflict is inevitable in an anarchic world. This worldview often leads to a self-fulfilling prophecy, where states act aggressively out of fear, creating the very conflicts they seek to avoid. Realism also overemphasizes military power and overlooks the growing influence of non-state actors, global institutions, and economic interdependence, which play crucial roles in shaping cooperation between states. Moreover, Realism’s state-centric approach often neglects long-term global challenges, such as climate change and pandemics, which require cooperative solutions beyond military power.

Liberalism, while offering a more optimistic view, can be overly idealistic, assuming that cooperation and mutual benefit will naturally prevail, even in the face of persistent self-interest and conflict. It often underestimates how states can act outside institutional norms, driven by power politics or national interests. Liberalism also tends toward naive interventionism, believing that the spread of democracy and liberal values will automatically lead to peace. This ignores the complex cultural and political realities of many nations, where efforts to impose liberal systems—as seen in Iraq and Afghanistan—can result in instability and unintended consequences. Additionally, Liberalism’s emphasis on economic interdependence creates vulnerabilities, where overreliance on globalization can undermine state sovereignty, particularly during economic crises.

Freedom offers a unifying ideal that bridges the gaps between Realism and Liberalism. While Realism focuses on survival and Liberalism on self-determination and well-being, Freedom en-compasses survival and power, but goes further by emphasizing the conditions that allow individuals and communities to flourish. While states must ensure security, they also have a responsibility to meet the aspirations and needs of their citizens.

The Emanation of Freedom

This concept allows for a more dynamic understanding of international relations. For instance, if the survival of a state is ensured but its citizens are oppressed or their desires for self-determination are ignored, can we truly say that the state is fulfilling its primary purpose? Freedom posits that the legitimacy of a state is not solely based on its ability to defend itself, but also on its ability to provide for the needs and aspirations of its people.

Freedom also accounts for the fluid nature of political entities. Unlike Realism, which often views the state as a permanent and unchanging actor, the concept of Freedom allows for the possibility that states may split, merge, or transform in response to the evolving needs and desires of their populations.

This fluidity is more aligned with historical realities—where empires have risen and fallen, borders have shifted, and new nations have emerged. Consider the dissolution of the Soviet Union or the peaceful separation of Czechoslovakia into the Czech Republic and Slovakia. These events illustrate that the survival of a nation-state is not the only—or even the primary—measure of its success. Instead, the ability of a political entity to adapt to the changing needs of its people, to allow for the expression of Freedom in its many forms, is a more accurate reflection of its legitimacy and strength.
