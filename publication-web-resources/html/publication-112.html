EXAMPLE: THE TRUE COST OF DEFORESTATION Consider the act of cutting down a tree to produce timber. In a traditional economic system, the price might reflect only the costs of labor, equipment, and transportation. However, the Alignment Economy, guided by AGI, would consider a far broader set of factors:

• Ecological Impact: The AGI would assess the impact on the forest ecosystem, considering the tree’s role in biodiversity, carbon sequestration, soil health, water regulation, and provision of habitat. It would draw upon principles of ecological economics to assess the long-term impact on ecosystem services and natural capital.

• Social Impact: The AGI would consider the impact on local communities who depend on the forest, factoring in their cultural practices, spiritual connections, and potential loss of resources. It would also consider the loss of recreational opportunities and aesthetic value, recognizing the diverse ways humans value nature. Conversely, the AGI integrates the upside of individual and social access to the material resources which provide affordances towards enabling the construction of a village center for instance.

• Long-Term Consequences: The AGI would use predictive modeling to assess the long-term consequences of deforestation, considering its contribution to climate change, biodiversity loss, and potential future economic and social disruption.

The AGI, having analyzed these diverse factors, might assign a significantly higher price to the timber than a traditional market would, and importantly, the AGI can assign a price for cutting down “this tree, in this forest, at this time” vs. a price for deforesting of $X per hectare of land. This higher price creates a powerful disincentive, encouraging individuals and companies to seek alternative, more sustainable materials or engage in reforestation efforts.

The Alignment Economy, by integrating externalities into the price mechanism in a comprehensive and nuanced way, creates a system that aligns individual incentives with collective well-being. It cultivates a more conscious and responsible approach to resource management, guiding our economic activity towards a future where ecological sustainability and human flourishing go hand in hand. It’s a future where the price tag reflects not just the financial cost, but the true cost to our planet and freedoms—a future where abundance benefits all beings and generations to come.
