Eastern philosophical and religious traditions offer rich perspectives on the concept of Freedom that complement and expand upon Western ideas. In Buddhism, freedom is understood as liberation from suffering and the cycle of rebirth. This is achieved through the realization of the Four Noble Truths and the practice of the Eightfold Path, which leads to the cessation of craving and attachment. True freedom, in the Buddhist view, is not merely the absence of external constraints but the attainment of inner peace and equanimity in the face of life’s challenges.

While elementary schools such as Hinayana seek individual liberation, some schools such as Mahayana seek collective liberation. More exclusive schools such as Vajrayana seek the eminence of liberation in a way that can be secularly interpreted as aligning with our notion of Freedom. The Bodhisattva in Mahayana Buddhism, who pledges to refrain from entering final nirvana until liberating all other beings through perpetual rebirth along thousands of lifetimes, exemplifies this commitment to collective freedom.

In Hinduism, freedom is associated with the realization of the true self (Atman) and its unity with the ultimate reality (Brahman). This is achieved through the practice of yoga and the cultivation of wisdom and detachment. Freedom, in this context, is not merely the ability to do as one pleases but the liberation from the illusion of the ego and the attainment of a state of pure consciousness. More exclusive schools such as Tantra seek the eminence of liberation in ways that parallel our secular notion of Freedom.

Taoism offers another perspective on freedom, understanding it as the ability to align oneself with the natural flow of the Tao and to act in harmony with the principles of the universe. This is achieved through the practice of wu-wei, or effortless action, and the cultivation of simplicity, spontaneity, and inner peace. Freedom, in the Taoist view, is not merely the absence of constraints but the ability to live in accordance with one’s true nature. The Tao Te Ching emphasizes that the Tao’s signal power resides specifically in endless possibility—the “ten thousand things” emerging in constant creativity streaming from the source.

Shinto, the indigenous faith of Japan, offers yet another perspective on freedom. In Shinto, freedom is closely tied to the concept of harmony with nature and the spiritual world. The Shinto worldview sees the divine (kami) as present in all aspects of the natural world. Freedom is understood as living in accor-dance with the natural order and maintaining proper relationships with the kami. This involves purification rituals, reverence for nature, and the cultivation of a harmonious community. The Shinto concept of freedom emphasizes the interconnectedness of all beings and the importance of maintaining balance in one’s relationships with others, na-ture, and the spiritual realm.
